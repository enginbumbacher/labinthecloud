When light hits a Rhodopsin, and when this light is brigher than the activation brightness the Rhodopsin releases its signalling molecule. If not, it won't release it. Once in a while, with low probability, the Rhodopsin releases the molecule randomly, even if it does not detect any light. They change their activation brightness over multiple seconds . The bigger the difference of the activation brightness and the general brightness, the longer it takes the Rhodopsins to adapt their activation brightness to the general brightness.

The signalling molecules travel from the photosensor to the flagellum base. In the meantime, the rhodopsins are regenerating the molecules they released. For each gate that is open for a certain duration, X signaling molecules per second come in.
Include image
When signalling molecules reach the base of the flagellum, they trigger a change in how the flagellum moves. This change leads to a change in direction of movement, for as long as signalling molecules arrive at the base. When there are no incoming signalling molecules anymore, the flagellum resumes normal behavior.
